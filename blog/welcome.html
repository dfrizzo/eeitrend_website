Field Notes

Why I'm Writing

May 13, 2026 · 3 min read
Leadership Thought Leadership

After more than two decades leading global engineering organizations through transformations, mergers, and platform rewrites, I've decided to start putting some of what I've learned into the open. This is the why.

Most of what I know about leading technology organizations didn't come from books. It came from running them — through private-equity transitions, post-merger integrations, mainframe-to-cloud rebuilds, and the slow, unglamorous work of changing how thousands of engineers ship software together.

I've been fortunate to do that work at LogRhythm, Exabeam, BMC, and Compuware, alongside teams across the U.S. and India. Along the way I've accumulated opinions — some hard-won, some still forming — about what actually moves the needle when you're trying to scale engineering, integrate acquisitions, hold quality bars, and adopt AI without losing your nerve.

This space is for those opinions.

What you'll find here

I plan to write about the things I wish more executives wrote candidly about:

  • Post-merger engineering integration — what's actually hard, what executives underestimate, and what holding attrition to single digits costs.
  • AI inside the SDLC — adopting it across code, review, testing, CI/CD, and support, with guardrails that hold up to enterprise scrutiny.
  • SaaS transformation in companies that started on-prem — what it takes operationally, financially, and culturally to make the shift stick.
  • The mainframe — still running the world's most critical workloads, and still surprisingly underserved by modern engineering thinking.
  • Engineering leadership under private-equity ownership — operating with clarity about cost, growth, and time horizons.

Who this is for

If you're a CTO, VP of Engineering, board member, or operating partner trying to make decisions in environments where the trade-offs are real and the stakes are high, you're who I'm writing for. I'll aim for posts that are specific, useful, and free of the airy abstractions that dominate so much executive writing online.

The point isn't to be right about everything. It's to be specific enough that other people can disagree productively.

I'd rather publish a piece that's wrong in interesting ways than a piece that's safely vague.

Cadence

I won't promise weekly. I will promise that when I publish, the piece will have earned its place — meaning I've either lived through what I'm describing, or I've thought hard enough about it that the writing surfaces something worth your time.

If something I write here is useful to you, or if it's wrong in a way you want to push back on, I'd love to hear from you. The most useful conversations I've had in this work have always come from disagreement, not agreement.
